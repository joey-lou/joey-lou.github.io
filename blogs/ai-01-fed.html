Where Does the Fed Get Its Money?

Where does the Federal Reserve get money to buy trillions in bonds?

The answer: they create it.

When the Fed buys a \$1 billion Treasury bond from a bank: 1. Fed adds the bond to its Assets (+\$1B) 2. Fed credits the bank's reserve account → Liabilities (+\$1B)

No vault of cash required. Just entries in a ledger. The Fed is the monopoly issuer of dollars.

This is what people mean by "printing money" — though no actual printing occurs. The constraint isn't technical; it's confidence. Create too much, and people stop trusting the currency.

The Money Family Tree

Not all money is created equal. There's a hierarchy:

Type What It Is Size
M0 (Monetary Base) Currency + Bank Reserves (Fed liabilities) ~\$6T
M1 M0 + Checking deposits ~\$18T
M2 M1 + Savings, CDs, Money Markets ~\$21T

The Fed only directly creates M0. Commercial banks multiply it through lending via fractional reserve banking. Every \$100 in reserves can become \$1,000 in deposits through successive rounds of lending.

When the Fed expands the monetary base, banks amplify that expansion through the credit creation process.

Velocity: The Speed of Money

Here's a puzzle: US GDP is ~\$29 trillion, but M2 is only ~\$21 trillion. How does \$21T support \$29T of economic activity?

The answer is velocity — each dollar gets spent multiple times per year. The equation is elegant:

$$M \times V = GDP$$

So velocity ≈ 1.4, meaning each dollar "turns over" about 1.4 times annually.

The problem? This used to be 2.0+ in the 1990s. Velocity has been declining.

Why? - Wealth inequality: Money concentrates with those who save rather than spend - Aging demographics: Older populations tend to save more - Financialization: Money flows into assets rather than the real economy - Low rates: Reduced penalty for holding cash - Precautionary savings: Uncertainty encourages hoarding

This is why the Fed could expand the money supply dramatically after 2008 and 2020 without immediate hyperinflation. Much of the new money sat idle.

The corollary: if velocity rebounds, that dormant money activates at once — potentially triggering inflation.

A Brief History of Fiat Money

The Fed's unconstrained money-creation powers are historically unprecedented — and only about 50 years old.

The Gold Standard Era (1879-1971)

Under the gold standard, the Fed was constrained: - Had to maintain ~40% gold backing for currency - Couldn't just "credit accounts" — needed physical gold - Gold supply is finite → money supply is limited

This was a feature, not a bug. It prevented governments from debasing currency. Prices barely moved from 1800 to 1900.

But it had a flaw: limited flexibility during crises. When the economy needed liquidity, the gold constraint limited the Fed's options.

Where Did the Fed Get Gold?

  1. Member banks deposited reserves when joining the Fed (1913)
  2. WWI gold inflows — Europe paid for American goods in gold
  3. FDR's gold confiscation (1933) — the big one

Executive Order 6102 made private gold ownership illegal. Citizens surrendered their gold at \$20.67/oz. The government then revalued it to \$35/oz — a 69% devaluation of dollar-denominated savings.

Nixon's "Temporary" Suspension (1971)

By the 1960s, America was running out of gold. France kept demanding gold for their dollars. Reserves were draining.

On August 15, 1971, Nixon announced a "temporary suspension" of gold convertibility. That suspension is now 54 years old and counting.

The dollar became pure faith: "This is valuable because we say so, and everyone believes it."

The Fed's Power Over Time

The Fed hasn't always been the powerful institution it is today. Its authority has fluctuated significantly.

The Benjamin Strong Era (1914-1928): One Man's Fed

The early Fed was decentralized — 12 regional banks with real autonomy. But NY Fed Governor Benjamin Strong dominated through sheer competence and force of personality. He was basically the Fed.

Then he died in October 1928. One year before the crash.

The Leaderless Years (1929-1933): Policy Paralysis

Without Strong, the Fed fragmented. Regional banks disagreed. No one had authority to override.

The Fed tightened into the Depression. Yes, really. Money supply collapsed 30%. Banks failed in waves. The result was catastrophic.

FDR Takes Over (1933-1951): Fed Becomes a Puppet

Roosevelt didn't just strip the Fed of gold — he stripped it of power. The Treasury ran monetary policy. The Fed became a printing press for war finance, required to keep rates pegged at 2.5% regardless of conditions.

"Independent" Fed was a polite fiction.

The Accord (1951): Independence Restored

After WWII, the Fed finally pushed back. The 1951 Treasury-Fed Accord restored Fed independence to set interest rates. The beginning of the modern era.

Volcker (1979-1987): The Fed Asserts Independence

Paul Volcker raised rates to 20% to crush inflation, despite political pressure. Unemployment hit 10%. He faced significant public backlash.

He succeeded. Inflation fell. And the Fed demonstrated it could act independently of political pressures.

The Greenspan-Bernanke-Powell Era: Expanded Authority

Each crisis expanded Fed authority: - 1987: Emergency rate cuts - 2008: QE, MBS purchases, emergency lending facilities - 2020: Corporate bonds, municipal backstops, broad market support

The balance sheet expanded from \$900B (2007) to \$9T (2022). Each crisis brought new powers that were never fully relinquished.

The Great Depression Debate

Two major schools of thought on the Fed's role:

Team Monetarist (Friedman, Bernanke)

"The Fed caused the Depression by not injecting liquidity"

The crash was survivable. But the Fed tightened, banks failed, money supply collapsed. If they'd acted as lender of last resort, it would've been a bad recession, not a decade of misery.

Bernanke acknowledged this at Friedman's 90th birthday: "You're right, we did it. We're very sorry. We won't do it again."

Team Austrian (Hayek, Rothbard)

"The Fed caused the bubble in the 1920s — the crash was the cure"

Strong's Fed kept rates too low in the 1920s, fueling speculation and malinvestment. 1929 was the market correcting distortions. Intervention would just delay the inevitable and make it worse.

Who's Right?

Both, kind of.

The bubble shouldn't have been blown (Austrian critique ✓). But once blown, the Fed's response made it worse (Monetarist critique ✓).

The modern Fed learned only half the lesson: - 1920s lesson: "Don't blow bubbles" → IGNORED - 1930s lesson: "Don't let credit collapse" → FOLLOWED AGGRESSIVELY

Result: serial bubble-blowing with aggressive crash response. Each rescue creates expectations. Each bubble gets bigger. The bill keeps growing.

The 2000 Exception: When Bubbles Pop Cleanly

The dot-com crash was different. The NASDAQ fell 78%. The S&P 500 dropped 49%. Yet the recession was mild — eight months, with unemployment peaking at 6.3%.

Why did this work when 2008 was catastrophic?

It was an equity bubble, not a credit bubble.

2000 2008
VCs lost money Banks became insolvent
Stocks went to zero Mortgages went underwater
No leverage, no contagion Debt everywhere, cascading defaults

When equity holders lose money, they absorb the loss — painful but contained. When credit defaults, it cascades through the system. Banks fail, credit freezes, and the real economy contracts.

In 2000, investors lost money but didn't default on loans. Banks stayed solvent. The system kept functioning.

The catch: The Fed's response (rates to 1%, held too long) inflated the housing bubble that caused 2008. Even in "success," the seeds of the next crisis were planted.

The Modern Dilemma

So here we are. The Fed is more powerful than ever — and more trapped than ever.

The trap: - Asset prices depend on low rates - Corporate debt needs cheap refinancing - Financial system holds massive bond portfolios - Markets expect rescue ("Fed Put")

Each bubble gets bigger. Each intervention gets larger. The endgame scenarios:

  1. Muddle Through: Low growth forever, Japan-style stagnation
  2. Inflate Away: Let inflation run hot, stealth default on debt
  3. Volcker 2.0: Finally allow pain, deep recession, reset
  4. Loss of Confidence: Dollar crisis, forced austerity

No one knows which path will unfold. The current approach of perpetual intervention may be sustainable, or it may not be.

The Bottom Line

  • The Fed creates money through accounting entries — it works as long as confidence holds
  • Money supply = Fed's base $\times$ bank multiplication $\times$ velocity
  • Gold standard constrained money creation; fiat removed all external limits
  • Fed power has fluctuated wildly — it's only been "omnipotent" since ~1971
  • The Depression debate: both the bubble AND the response were policy errors
  • 2000 shows bubbles CAN pop cleanly — but only equity bubbles, not credit bubbles
  • Modern bubbles are credit-entangled; clean pops are unlikely
  • The Fed is trapped: can't let it crash, can't stop inflating

In short: fiat money is a system built on confidence, and the Fed's job is to maintain that confidence while navigating an increasingly complex set of constraints.
